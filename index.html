Recipe for a good joke

A Data-Driven Look Inside the New Yorker Caption Contest Laughter

By Chi Vo

More than 4,000 captions for just one contest. Across 385 contests and 2.3 million votes, the verdict is clear: while judges may disagree, the ingredients of humor remain surprisingly consistent.

Funny = Brevity + Surprise + Truth

From the dataset, human-written captions are rated—across the categories of "funny," "somewhat funny," and "not funny." While responses span the spectrum, one thing stands out: truly funny votes are rare. People are tough critics—and getting a funny vote is not an easy feat.

Two million captions later and the verdict is in: most captions barely get a chuckle. The majority sit just above "not funny," with only a tiny sliver reaching what you'd call genuinely funny. The curve drops off fast, like a bad punchline. Turns out, writing a caption that makes everyone laugh is incredibly rare. Even in a contest built for humor, the average score is closer to a sigh than a laugh.

After analyzing the structure of captions, a subtle signal lays in punctuation. Captions ending in question marks slightly underperformed compared to those ending in periods or with no punctuation at all. The best jokes don’t always ask—they tell.

Captions with fewer words tended to score higher in funniness. The sweet spot is at 50 characters. Brevity helps but cannot guarantee a funny vote.
